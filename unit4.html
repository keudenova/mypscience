🦖 Mission 1: Ancient Swamps

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Crude oil started as tiny sea organisms — plankton and algae — millions of years ago. Buried under mud, heat, and pressure for millions of years, their remains slowly transformed into fossil fuels.

This is why crude oil is called a fossil fuel — it's literally made from fossilised ancient life, and it takes millions of years to form (much faster than we use it up).

Crude oil isn't one single substance — it's a mixture of thousands of different hydrocarbons: molecules made only of hydrogen and carbon atoms, in chains of different lengths.

Short chains (like methane, 1 carbon) behave very differently from long chains (30+ carbons) — that difference is the whole basis of how we separate crude oil into useful products.

🧪 Mission 2: Naming Hydrocarbons

20 XP available

☆☆☆

The simplest hydrocarbons are alkanes — straight or branched chains where every carbon is joined by single bonds. The name changes based on chain length.

Each extra carbon added to the chain makes a new molecule with its own name.

CarbonsNameFormula
1MethaneCH₄
2EthaneC₂H₆
3PropaneC₃H₈
4ButaneC₄H₁₀
Notice the pattern: each formula is CnH2n+2. Try it — for 4 carbons: H = (2×4)+2 = 10. ✓

🏭 Mission 3: Distillation Tower

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Crude oil is heated until it vaporises, then fed into a tall tower. Different-length hydrocarbons condense back to liquid at different heights, because longer chains have higher boiling points.

Shorter, lighter hydrocarbons rise higher before condensing (low boiling point); longer, heavier ones condense near the bottom (high boiling point).

FractionApprox. chain lengthCommon use
Gases1–4 carbonsCooking gas, heating
Petrol5–10 carbonsCar fuel
Kerosene10–16 carbonsJet fuel
Diesel16–20 carbonsTrucks, trains
Fuel oil20–27 carbonsShips, power stations
Bitumen27+ carbonsRoads, roofing

🔬 Mission 4: Fraction Properties Lab

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Chain length doesn't just affect boiling point — it changes almost everything about how a fraction behaves.

PropertyShort chains (e.g. petrol)Long chains (e.g. bitumen)
ColourPale / colourlessDark
ViscosityRunny (flows easily)Thick, sticky
Ease of evaporationEvaporates easilyBarely evaporates
FlammabilityIgnites easilyHard to ignite
Flame sootinessClean flameSmoky, sooty flame
All of these trends have the same root cause: longer molecules have stronger intermolecular forces holding them together and tangled up, making them harder to move, evaporate, or burn cleanly.

✂️ Mission 5: Cracking Station

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Distillation gives us more long-chain fractions than people actually want, and not enough short chains like petrol. Cracking fixes that mismatch by breaking long chains into shorter, more useful ones.

Heat (and a catalyst) breaks one long-chain molecule into two smaller ones — typically a shorter alkane plus a small, reactive alkene.

Why bother? There's high demand for short-chain products (petrol, plastics feedstock) but crude oil naturally contains more long chains than we need. Cracking converts "too much of one thing" into "just enough of what's needed."

🔗 Mission 6: Polymer Chains

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Polymerisation is the joining of many small molecules (monomers) — often the small alkenes made by cracking — into one giant chain molecule (a polymer).

Like a paperclip chain — each monomer "clips" onto the next, forming one long polymer molecule.

PolymerMade fromCommon use
Polyethene (PE)EthenePlastic bags, bottles
Polypropene (PP)PropeneRopes, food containers
Polystyrene (PS)StyrenePackaging, insulation
PVCVinyl chloridePipes, window frames
LDPE (low-density polyethene) has loosely tangled, branched chains — soft and flexible, used for bags. HDPE (high-density) has tightly packed, straighter chains — rigid and strong, used for bottles and pipes.

🛍️ Mission 7: Plastic Properties

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Every plastic product is chosen for a reason — its properties match the job it needs to do.

🛍️ LDPE

Flexible, lightweight → carrier bags, cling film.

🚰 HDPE

Rigid, strong, chemical-resistant → pipes, bottles.

🪑 PP

Tough, heat-resistant → food containers, ropes.

📦 PS

Lightweight, can be foamed → packaging, insulation.

🚪 PVC

Durable, weatherproof → window frames, pipes.

🔍 Perspex

Transparent, shatter-resistant → windows, signs.

When choosing "the best plastic" for a product, always link back to a specific property — "flexible" or "waterproof" is a much stronger answer than just "it's a good material."

🌍 Mission 8: Material Choices

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Crude oil is a non-renewable resource — it took millions of years to form, and countries are already thinking hard about what happens when reserves run low or become harder to extract.

This connects directly to your unit's big idea: as environmental impact becomes a bigger concern, the demand for materials is changing — pushing industries to look for alternatives to traditional plastics.
ConcernWhy it matters
Resource securityOil reserves are finite and unevenly distributed between countries
Plastic pollutionMost plastics don't biodegrade, building up in oceans and landfill
Carbon emissionsExtracting and processing crude oil releases greenhouse gases
Possible alternatives being explored: bioplastics (made from plant starch), recycled plastics, and biodegradable packaging — each with its own trade-offs in cost, strength, and practicality. This is exactly the kind of trade-off your Criterion D debate will explore.
