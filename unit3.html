⚛️ Mission 1: Charge It Up 電荷

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Every atom is a tiny team of three particles. Electricity is really just about controlling one of them.

Protons (+) and neutrons sit in the nucleus; electrons (−) orbit around it. Protons and electrons carry equal and opposite charge.

ParticleChargeLocation
ProtonPositive (+)Nucleus
NeutronNo charge (neutral)Nucleus
ElectronNegative (−)Orbiting the nucleus
Electrons are the lightest and the only particle that can move between atoms — this is exactly what "electricity" is: electrons on the move.

🔦 Mission 2: Circuit Basics 電路

20 XP available

☆☆☆

To light a bulb you need two things: a cell (the energy source) and a complete, unbroken loop called a closed circuit. Break the loop anywhere and the bulb goes out.

This circuit is open — the switch breaks the loop, so no current can flow and the bulb stays off.

Conductor導體
Lets electric current pass through easily — metals like copper.
Insulator絕緣體
Blocks current — rubber, plastic, wood.
Switch開關
Opens or closes a circuit on purpose.
You'll need to recognise these circuit symbols: cell, battery, bulb, switch, ammeter, voltmeter, resistor, and rheostat — they're the "alphabet" of circuit diagrams.

⚠️ Mission 3: Short Circuit City 短路

20 XP available

☆☆☆

A short circuit happens when current finds a path with almost no resistance — skipping the bulb or appliance entirely.

Because there's barely any resistance in the shortcut, a very large current flows — and current heats wires up. That heat is the fire danger.

Safety deviceWhat it does
Fuse 保險絲A thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit if current gets too high
Circuit breaker 斷路器A switch that automatically flips off when current is too high — and can be reset
A large current has a heating effect on wires — this is exactly why short circuits are dangerous, and why fuses and circuit breakers exist.

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Two meters, two different jobs, two different ways of connecting them into a circuit.

An ammeter is wired into the circuit (in series) to measure current flowing through. A voltmeter is wired across a component (in parallel) to measure the voltage drop over it.

MeterMeasuresUnit
AmmeterCurrentAmpere, A
VoltmeterVoltageVolt, V
A battery with a greater voltage pushes harder — it causes a greater current to flow through the same circuit.

🧱 Mission 5: Resistance Zone 電阻

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Resistance is how much a component fights against current flowing through it. More resistance means less current gets through — like a narrower pipe letting through less water.

Cover what you want to find: V = I × R, I = V ÷ R, R = V ÷ I.

V = I × R
V — voltage, volts (V)
I — current, amps (A)
R — resistance, ohms (Ω)
Worked example

A circuit has a resistance of 5 Ω and a current of 2 A. What's the voltage?
V = I × R = 2 × 5 = 10 V

Rheostats are variable resistors — sliding them changes resistance on purpose, e.g. to dim a light or change a fan's speed.

🔗 Mission 6: Series Circuits 串聯電路

20 XP available

☆☆☆

In a series circuit, everything sits on one single loop — one after another, like beads on a string.

There's only one path, so the same current I flows through every point in the loop — including through both bulbs.

If one bulb in a series circuit breaks or is removed, the whole loop is broken — every bulb goes out. That's the big downside of series wiring.

🔀 Mission 7: Parallel Circuits 並聯電路

20 XP available

☆☆☆

In a parallel circuit, the current splits into separate branches — each with its own path back to the cell.

The current in the main loop is the sum of the branch currents. The voltage across each branch is the same.

SeriesParallel
Same current everywhereCurrent splits between branches
One bulb breaks → all go outOne bulb breaks → others stay on
Rarely used at homeUsed in home wiring
This is exactly why homes use parallel wiring: turning off (or blowing) one appliance shouldn't cut power to the whole house.

🛡️ Mission 8: Electric Safety

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Everything you've learned this unit connects to one practical goal: using electricity without getting hurt.

💧 Keep it dry

Water conducts electricity — never use electrical devices with wet hands or near water.

🔌 Don't overload

Too many devices on one socket can draw too much current and overheat the wiring.

🧵 Check the cable

Frayed or damaged insulation exposes bare wire — a shock and fire risk.

Every safety rule you'll ever see about electricity comes back to two ideas from this unit: water/damaged insulation lowers resistance (letting dangerous current flow where it shouldn't), and too much current generates too much heat.
