🕵️ Mission 1: Energy Detective

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Energy isn't something you can see or touch directly — but it's a measurable physical quantity, measured in joules (J). If something "has energy," it means it can do work. There are eight forms you need to recognise:

⚡ Electrical電能
Carried by electric current — a working bulb has it, a battery alone doesn't (that's chemical energy).
🏃 Kinetic動能
Energy of anything moving.
⬆️ Gravitational potential重力位能
Stored energy of something raised up.
🧵 Elastic potential彈性位能
Stored in something stretched or squashed.
🔥 Thermal熱能
Energy from temperature/hotness.
💡 Light光能
Carried in light rays, even ones we can't see.
🔊 Sound聲能
Carried by vibrations we hear.
🧪 Chemical化學能
Stored in fuel — petrol, food, an unlit match.

Energy always transforms rather than disappearing — a torch turns chemical energy into electrical, then into light and (wasted) heat.

🕵️ Detective habit: whenever you see a device working, ask "what form did the energy start as, and what form is it becoming?" That question answers almost every exam question in this unit.
🕵️ Detective case: A phone rings, vibrates, and lights up its screen while the battery slowly drains. How many forms of energy can you name in that one scene — before you look at the cases below?
Case 1: A ball rolls down a hill. Which energy is changing INTO kinetic energy?
Case 2: Coal burns and gives off heat and light. What kind of energy was stored in the coal?

🏗️ Mission 3: Sky Tower 重力位能

20 XP available

☆☆☆

You're a crane operator lifting materials up a skyscraper. The higher and heavier the load, the more Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE) it stores — energy that's ready to release the moment it falls.

The mass m sits at height h above the ground. If released, all that GPE converts into kinetic energy on the way down.

GPE = m × g × h
GPE — energy, in joules (J)
m — mass, in kilograms (kg)
g — gravity, 9.81 m/s² on Earth
h — height above ground, in metres (m)
Worked example — step by step

A crane lifts 1500 g of concrete to the top of a 20 m building. How much GPE does it gain?

Step 1 — list the given values: m = 1500 g, g = 9.81 m/s², h = 20 m
Step 2 — convert units: 1500 g = 1.5 kg
Step 3 — plug into the formula: GPE = 1.5 × 9.81 × 20
Step 4 — calculate: GPE = 294.3 J

Try it yourself (paper & pencil): John (50 kg) is suspended 50 m above the ground. How much GPE does he have?
A 75 kg crate is lifted 300 m up a tower. What's its GPE? (g = 9.81)
If you double an object's height (mass unchanged), what happens to its GPE?

🏎️ Mission 4: Speed Zone 動能

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Welcome to the Speed Zone. Every moving object carries Kinetic Energy (KE) — and speed matters far more than mass does.

The motion lines behind the block show it's already moving at speed v — that's what gives it kinetic energy.

KE = ½ × m × v²
KE — joules (J)
m — mass, kg
v — speed, m/s
Worked example — step by step

A 2 kg ball moves at 4 m/s. Find its KE.

Step 1: m = 2 kg, v = 4 m/s
Step 2: KE = ½ × 2 × 4²
Step 3: 4² = 16, so KE = ½ × 2 × 16
Step 4: KE = 16 J

Because v is squared, speed has a much bigger effect on KE than mass does — this is exactly why car crashes get so much more dangerous at higher speeds.
A car doubles its speed. What happens to its kinetic energy?
A 10 kg dog runs at 6 m/s. What's its KE?

♻️ Mission 5: Energy Never Dies 能量守恆定律

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Law of conservation of energy: energy can't be created or destroyed — only changed from one form to another. Total energy in an isolated system stays constant.

A swinging pendulum: energy swaps between GPE (at the highest points) and KE (at the bottom) — the total never changes.

For something falling or swinging freely (ignoring air resistance), GPE lost turns into KE gained:

ghi + ½vi² = ghf + ½vf²
Initial height/speed on the left, final height/speed on the right.
Worked example

The Longtan Dam is 261 m tall. Find the speed of water at the bottom (starting speed = 0).

Step 1: ghi = ½vf² (all GPE converts to KE)
Step 2: 9.81 × 261 = ½ × vf²
Step 3: 2560.4 = ½ × vf² → vf² = 5120.8
Step 4: vf = ≈ 71.6 m/s

A bouncy ball bounces lower and lower each time. Where does the "missing" energy actually go?
In an isolated system, what stays constant?

🧪 Mission 6: The Efficiency Lab 效率

20 XP available

☆☆☆

In the lab, every device you test wastes some energy — usually as heat. A Sankey diagram shows this visually: the width of each arrow shows how much energy it carries.

Straight arrow = useful output. Downward arrow = wasted energy. Here: 750 J in, only 150 J comes out as useful light — the rest is wasted heat.

Efficiency = useful output ÷ total input × 100%
Worked example

Using the diagram above: efficiency = 150 ÷ 750 × 100% = 20%

DeviceTypical efficiency
Filament lamp~2%
LED light~20%
Car engine~20%
Notice none of these are 100%. Every real device wastes some energy — mostly as heat. That's exactly why "more efficient systems" (this unit's big idea) matters so much to engineers.
Lab test: a device takes in 750 J and usefully outputs 150 J. Efficiency?

🔌 Mission 7: Power Grid

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Power stations can't create energy — they transform it. A typical fossil-fuel plant follows this chain:

Chemical → thermal → kinetic → electrical. Every arrow loses a little energy as waste heat along the way.

Renewable可再生能源
Naturally replenished — solar, wind, hydro, geothermal.
Non-renewable不可再生能源
Finite, formed over millions of years — coal, oil, gas.
Wind and hydro power skip the first two steps entirely — they start straight from kinetic energy (moving air or water), which is one reason they're considered cleaner: no combustion, no chemical-to-thermal step, no CO₂ from burning fuel.
Which energy source does NOT start its conversion chain with chemical energy?
Why do solar farms need much more land than a coal plant of similar output?

🔥 Mission 8: Heat Runners

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Heat is always racing from hot to cold — by three different routes:

Conduction — direct contact, hot end to cold end.

Convection — hot fluid rises, cool fluid sinks, in a loop.

Radiation — waves travel through empty space, no contact needed.

Conduction 傳導

Metals conduct well thanks to free electrons that carry heat quickly through the material.

Convection 對流

Hot fluid is less dense and rises; cooler fluid sinks to replace it — this loop is a convection current.

Radiation 輻射

Mainly infrared waves. The only method that works through a vacuum — this is how the Sun heats Earth.

A vacuum flask uses all three ideas at once: a double wall with vacuum in between (blocks conduction & convection) and a silvery inner surface (blocks radiation).
Which heat transfer method can travel through empty space (a vacuum)?
Why are metals good conductors of heat?
