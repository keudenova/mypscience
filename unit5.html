Natural selection — sometimes summed up as "survival of the fittest" — explains how species change over generations.

Individuals with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and pass those traits to offspring.

Both genetics and environment affect survival — a useful gene only helps if the environment actually rewards that trait. This is the heart of this unit's Statement of Inquiry: identity comes from a mix of internal (genetic) and environmental factors.

🌱 Mission 3: Mendel's Garden

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Gregor Mendel bred thousands of pea plants in the 1800s and discovered predictable patterns in how traits pass from parent to offspring — before anyone even knew what DNA was.

TermMeaning
GenotypeThe genetic makeup — the actual alleles, e.g. Bb
PhenotypeThe physical trait you can see, e.g. brown eyes
HomozygousTwo identical alleles, e.g. BB or bb
HeterozygousTwo different alleles, e.g. Bb
Dominant alleleShows up in the phenotype even with one copy (capital letter)
Recessive alleleOnly shows up if both copies are recessive (lowercase letter)
Memory trick: dominant alleles "dominate" — they only need one copy to control the phenotype. A recessive allele has to hide behind a dominant one unless it has no competition (i.e. it's paired with another recessive).

⬜ Mission 4: Punnett Squares

20 XP available

☆☆☆

A Punnett square predicts the possible genotypes (and their odds) of offspring from two parents.

Bb × Bb gives a 1 : 2 : 1 ratio — 1 BB, 2 Bb, 1 bb — so 3 in 4 offspring show the dominant trait, 1 in 4 shows the recessive.

How to build one

Step 1: Write one parent's alleles across the top, the other's down the side.
Step 2: Fill each box with the combination of the row + column allele.
Step 3: Count the genotypes to get ratios; use dominant/recessive rules to get phenotype ratios.

🔍 Mission 5: Test Cross Detective

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Here's a genetics mystery: an organism shows the dominant phenotype — but is its genotype BB or Bb? You can't tell just by looking. A test cross solves it.

The detective method

Step 1: Cross the mystery organism with a known homozygous recessive (bb).
Step 2: Look at the offspring.
Step 3a: If ALL offspring show the dominant trait → mystery parent was BB.
Step 3b: If SOME offspring show the recessive trait → mystery parent was Bb.

Why does this work? BB × bb can only ever make Bb offspring (all dominant-looking). But Bb × bb gives a 1:1 mix of Bb and bb — so any recessive offspring at all proves the mystery parent must carry a hidden recessive allele.

🌀 Mission 6: DNA Structure

20 XP available

☆☆☆

DNA is shaped like a twisted ladder — a double helix. Watson and Crick are usually credited with figuring out this structure in 1953, but they relied heavily on X-ray images taken by Rosalind Franklin.

Two strands twist around each other; the "rungs" connecting them are pairs of chemical bases.

The bases always pair the same way: A with T, and C with G. This fixed pairing is what makes DNA replication and reading genetic code possible.

📋 Mission 7: DNA Replication

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Before a cell divides, it must copy its DNA exactly — otherwise the new cells wouldn't have complete instructions.

The double helix "unzips," and each old strand acts as a template to build a new matching strand — so each new DNA molecule has one old strand and one new one.

Because base pairing is fixed (A–T, C–G), each old strand can only be copied one correct way — this is what makes replication so accurate.

🔬 Mission 8: Cell Division

20 XP available

☆☆☆

Cells aren't always dividing — most spend the majority of their time doing their normal job. When division does happen, it's one of two very different types.

Mitosis — 1 cell → 2 identical cells. Happens in all body cells, for growth and repair.

Meiosis — 1 cell → 4 different cells with half the chromosomes. Only in gamete-producing cells.
